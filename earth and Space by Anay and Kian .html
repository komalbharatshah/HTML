Formation of the Solar System

The Solar System formed around 4.6 billion years ago from the collapse of a massive molecular cloud known as the solar nebula. As the cloud collapsed, it began to spin and flatten into a rotating disk. At the center, gravity caused the Sun to ignite, while the remaining gas and dust particles formed the planets, moons, and other bodies.

Inner planets like Earth and Venus formed from heavy elements, while outer planets like Jupiter and Saturn formed further away with lighter gases. The process of accretion created the planets we see today, gradually growing larger over millions of years.

Formation of the Moon

The Moon is believed to have formed about 4.5 billion years ago after a collision between Earth and a Mars-sized body called Theia. The impact sent debris into space, which eventually came together to form the Moon.

This event also tilted Earth’s axis and stabilized its rotation, which helped create the conditions necessary for life to thrive. Over billions of years, the Moon’s gravitational pull has shaped the tides and affected Earth's climate.
